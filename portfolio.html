[01] // CLASS REFLECTION

What this class taught me.

Reflection

The hardest part of this project was getting the design right before we built it. Once the geometry and the load path were worked out on paper, the build itself moved fast. The lesson was clear: time spent in planning compounds, and time skipped in planning shows up later as a snapped arm, a bent track, or a launch that misses by ten feet. Working in a team of four also forced me to defend design choices instead of just sketching them, and that pressure produced a stronger final concept than any of us would have made alone. Cardboard turned out to be a good teacher because it fails fast and forces you to see the problem.

Moving Forward

The single skill I am taking out of this class is documenting the design process as I go, not after the fact. On this project we sometimes had to reconstruct decisions from memory, and that wasted time and lost detail. In my next ME courses, especially in CAD work and lab reports, I am going to log decisions, sketches, and test results in the moment so documentation becomes a byproduct of the work instead of an extra task. The same habit applies to capstone, internship work, and any real engineering project where the record matters as much as the result.

[02] // PROJECT

The Capitalist.

TYPE
Ballista (tension-powered)
TEAM
3 members
POWER SOURCE
Rubber band
METRICS
Distance, Accuracy, Speed, Creativity

The Capitalist is a ballista-style catapult built from cardboard and powered by a rubber band stretched between two dowel rods. The design uses a triangular frame to resist recoil and distribute launch force through the base, with a sliding track that releases the projectile at a 45 degree launch angle. It was tested against two projectile types, a sack ball and a ping pong ball, and evaluated on distance, accuracy, speed, and creativity.

[03] // BRAINSTORMING

Three sketches, one direction.

The team generated three distinct concepts before committing to a build direction. Each sketch introduced a specific improvement over the last: stability, force distribution, and finally maneuverability. We chose the direction that balanced structural longevity against build complexity.

Initial Sketch — Basic Ballista

First concept. Took the core characteristics of a ballista and translated them to paper. Colton introduced this direction based on the ballista having the strongest overall statistics across range, accuracy, and speed compared to other catapult archetypes.

"Incorporating feet into the base allows for more stability and longevity. Previous year designs failed on the longevity of their build."

Second Iteration — Force Distribution

Second iteration targeted a specific failure mode from the first concept: the arms snapping after launch. The fix was a 45 degree triangle connecting the axle to the base, spreading load across a wider footprint and adding a track holder at the bottom.

"Adding a track holder at the bottom allows for more longevity and distribution on the catapult. The dowel rod goes through the track."

Additional Sketch — Full Maneuverability

Third concept tried to account for every failure mode we could name. It would have been the most complex and time consuming build, but also the one with the strongest launch and widest targeting range. We treated it as a direction to borrow ideas from rather than a build target.

"This catapult would be able to swivel left and right, up and down. It would have the most targeting angles compared to competitive designs."

[04] // PROTOTYPE

First build.

The prototype pulled the strongest elements from each sketch: the triangular frame from the second iteration, the base footprint from the first, and selected details from the third. Building revealed problems no sketch could predict. The sack ball's friction against the track was worse than expected and caused inconsistent launches, and the rubber band anchor points shifted under load. Both issues drove design changes documented in the "Lessons Learned" section.

[05] // INKSCAPE DESIGN

Vector design.

Inkscape is a free, open source vector graphics editor used to create the cut files the laser cutter reads. We used it to lay out all the structural components of The Capitalist as flat vector paths — the track, the triangular frame sides, the base, and the slot joints that lock the pieces together without glue. Every line in the file corresponds to a physical cut in the material, so dimensions had to be exact. The design is labeled "THE CAPITALIST" in the file and was exported directly to the laser cutter.

[06] // CUT

Laser cutter.

CUTTER IN ACTION
CUT PARTS

The laser cutter reads the Inkscape vector file and burns cut lines through the material with a focused beam. We cut on wood sheet stock, which gave the final build significantly more structural rigidity than the cardboard prototype. The key thing we learned using the cutter is that kerf — the small amount of material the beam removes on each cut — has to be accounted for in the design file, or joints that are supposed to fit together will come out either too tight or too loose. Test cuts before the final run saved us from that problem.

[09] // LESSONS LEARNED

What the build taught us.

01

The Power Source

Running two dowel rods with the rubber band stretched between them allowed for a longer pull without damaging the structure. The upper dowel also gave the launcher a cleaner release point than a single anchor would have.

02

The Sliding Track

The sack ball's friction on the bare cardboard track was a major source of inconsistency. We added a removable sliding sheet on top of the track to reduce friction and force a more linear launch path.

03

Projectile Physics

The sack ball deforms in flight, which produced irregular trajectories and high trial-to-trial variance. The ping pong ball held its shape and launched to nearly identical distances under the same conditions.

04

Structural Geometry

The triangle is the shape that best absorbs recoil and carries the weight of the projectile back through the frame. Triangular bracing was the single largest factor in the catapult surviving repeated launches.

[10] // LIMITATIONS & FUTURE DIRECTION

What we would do differently.

Problems Identified

  • Track durability — cardboard bent and degraded over repeated launches causing misfires
  • Rubber band mount position limited effective pullback length
  • Sack ball deformed and misfired without a containment point on the track
  • Overall material — cardboard could not hold up across a full competition day

Improvements

  • Build with sturdier materials — wood or acrylic instead of cardboard for longevity
  • Mount rubber bands further along the dowel rods for longer, more consistent pullback
  • Improve track durability to prevent misfires on repeated launches
  • Add a sling or basket to the track to prevent sack ball misfires on launch
