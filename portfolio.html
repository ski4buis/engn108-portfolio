[01] // PROBLEM STATEMENT

What we set out to solve.

The challenge was to design and build a catapult evaluated across four metrics: range, accuracy, distance, and creativity. The team selected a ballista architecture for its historical precedent in accuracy and rapid release, then engineered a build that could be tested under controlled conditions to isolate performance variables.

The Capitalist is the team's solution: a tension-band ballista with a triangular structural frame, a guided slider track, and a fixed launch angle of 26.6 degrees to keep release geometry constant across trials. The full project moved through three brainstorming iterations, a cardboard prototype, an Inkscape vector design, a laser-cut balsa wood final build, and a measured test campaign across two projectile types.

The Capitalist is a tension-band ballista built from laser-cut balsa wood. The design uses a triangular frame to resist recoil, a guided slider track to constrain the projectile's path, and a fixed-angle release at 26.6 degrees to control launch geometry. It was tested across two projectile types over multiple trials, with distance, time, and speed recorded for each launch.

[03] // BRAINSTORMING

Three sketches, one direction.

The team produced three distinct concepts before committing to a build direction. Each sketch introduced a specific improvement over the last: stability, force distribution, then maneuverability. We chose the direction that balanced structural longevity against build complexity, with Colton's initial ballista concept setting the foundation.

Sketch 01 — Basic Ballista Concept

First Concept

The team's first concept took the core characteristics of a ballista and translated them to paper. The ballista was selected because it produces the strongest combined statistics across range, accuracy, and speed compared to other catapult archetypes. The previous year's ballista build had failed on structural longevity, so this concept introduced a wider footed base to address that.

"Incorporating feet into the base allows for more stability and longevity. Previous year designs failed on the longevity of their build."

Sketch 02 — Force Distribution

Second Iteration

The second iteration targeted a specific failure mode from the first: arms snapping after launch. The fix was a 45 degree triangle connecting the axle to the base, spreading load across a wider footprint and adding a track holder at the bottom to better distribute energy on release.

"Adding a track holder at the bottom allows for more longevity and distribution on the catapult. The dowel rod goes through the track."

Sketch 03 — Full Maneuverability

Third Concept

The third concept tried to account for every failure mode we could name. It would have been the most complex and time-consuming build, but also the one with the strongest launch and widest targeting range. We treated it as a direction to borrow ideas from rather than a build target, scrapping it due to complexity and limited material resources.

"This catapult would be able to swivel left and right, up and down. It would have the most targeting angles compared to competitive designs."

[04] // DECISION MATRIX

Choosing the path forward.

After the initial cardboard build, the team evaluated two structural solutions before finalizing the design. Each was tested against the longevity and load-distribution failures we observed in the first build.

Solution 01

Prioritized stability by elongating the triangle base connectors, increasing weight and longevity, and removing the original track. The change made the frame more rigid but it sounded better on paper than it performed in testing.

Solution 02

Added an additional base, thickened the triangle base connectors, and inserted a popsicle stick at the front to reduce strain and decrease tension on the frame. This is the configuration that carried into the final laser-cut build.

[05] // PROTOTYPE

Cardboard build.

The cardboard prototype combined the strongest elements from each sketch: the triangular frame from the second iteration, the base footprint from the first, and selected details from the third. Specifications: 25 degree incline, 25 degree depression. Building revealed problems no sketch could predict. The bean bag's friction against the cardboard track was worse than expected and caused inconsistent launches, and the rubber band anchor points shifted under load. Both findings drove the move to laser-cut wood for the final build, where the slick surface reduced track friction and the rigid frame held the rubber band geometry constant under tension.

[06] // INKSCAPE DESIGN

Vector design for the laser cutter.

Inkscape is a free, open-source vector graphics editor used to produce the cut files the laser cutter reads. We used it to lay out every structural component as flat vector paths: the track, the triangular frame sides, the base, and the slot joints that lock pieces together without glue. Every line in the file corresponds to a physical cut in the material, so dimensions had to be exact. The file is labeled "THE CAPITALIST" and was exported directly to the laser cutter.

[11] // ANALYSIS

What the data tells us.

Chart 01 — Distance: Bean Bag vs Ping Pong

The ping pong ball consistently outperformed the bean bag across all three trials, averaging 320.33 in vs 90.33 in — a 3.5x difference. This result is consistent with projectile physics: the lighter ping pong ball experiences a higher acceleration-to-mass ratio from the same elastic tension force. The bean bag, being significantly heavier, requires more energy to achieve equivalent distance. Test 3 produced peak distances for both projectiles (217 in bean bag, 354 in ping pong), suggesting maximum tension band calibration in that round. The high variance in bean bag results (16 to 217 in) points to inconsistent release mechanics rather than a design flaw, since the ping pong results were more stable (283 to 354 in).

Chart 02 — Accuracy: 5 ft vs 10 ft Target

At the 5 ft target, the team scored consistently (15, 10, 15), averaging 13.33. At the 10 ft target, performance dropped significantly (0, 10, 10), averaging 6.67. This degradation with increased distance is expected: the ballista was optimized for repeatability at close range, where the high-tension band system delivers sufficient accuracy. At 10 ft, minor deviations in release angle and band pre-tension compound over a longer trajectory, widening the spread from the target. The Test 1 score of 0 at 10 ft indicates the launch was likely undertensioned or the angle was off, a finding that would improve with a calibrated release mechanism.

Design Implications

The data supports choosing the ballista for accuracy-priority applications at close range. For distance-focused performance, the design's tension band configuration favors lighter projectiles. Future optimization should target consistent band pre-load (mechanical locking vs freehand pull) and a guided release trigger to reduce human-variable error between shots.

[12] // PROBLEMS IDENTIFIED

Mapped to all rubric categories.

Problems were identified across all five rubric categories: Accuracy, Distance, Speed, Creativity, and Weight. Each problem is paired with its corresponding improvement.

Accuracy
Problem

Performance dropped from 13.33 average at 5 ft to 6.67 average at 10 ft. Inconsistency at distance was the primary accuracy problem, traced to shot-to-shot variance in band pre-tension and hand-loaded release.

Improvement

Calibrated mechanical release trigger to standardize pre-load, eliminating human-variable error between shots.

Distance
Problem

Bean bag distances ranged from 16 to 217 inches across only three trials, a 13.5x spread on a single projectile. The launch system could not deliver repeatable energy transfer.

Improvement

Mount rubber bands further along the dowel rods to increase the effective moment arm and deliver greater launch energy per unit of elastic deformation.

Speed
Problem

Bean bag speeds ranged from 34.78 in/s (Test 2) to 169.53 in/s (Test 3), a nearly 5x variance. This mirrors the distance inconsistency and confirms the energy-delivery problem is at the launch mechanism, not the projectile or the operator.

Improvement

Add pre-tension calibration markings on the slider track so each shot pulls back to the same draw distance, eliminating the speed variance observed.

Creativity
Problem

The ballista design followed prior-year patterns rather than introducing meaningful innovation. Selecting a proven design was strategically correct, but we did not iterate on creative variations such as a sliding counterweight, multi-stage tension, or a guide rail, any of which could have differentiated our build.

Improvement

Add an adjustable angle bracket on the rear pivot allowing the launch angle to be tuned per test, plus a guide rail along the track to constrain projectile yaw.

Weight
Problem

At 182.39g the catapult is light enough to be portable, but the lightweight wood frame deformed under repeated firing stress, contributing to wear and accuracy drift observed in late-round tests.

Improvement

Reinforce high-stress joints (cross bar to dowel rod connectors) with denser hardwood or metal brackets without significantly increasing total mass, addressing structural deformation under cyclic load.

[13] // FUTURE ADJUSTMENTS

Three structural fixes.

Beyond the rubric-mapped improvements, three specific structural adjustments came out of post-test review. Each is documented with the photo from the post-mortem.

Solution 01

Top Dowel Rod

Add an additional dowel rod on the top-front side of the track. Forms a structure similar to a football goal post and improves the trajectory by giving the rubber band a cleaner, higher release point.

Solution 02

Narrower Base

Lower the width of the base. The wider base caused interference during launch, and trimming it back lets the projectile clear the frame more cleanly.

Solution 03

Shorter Triangle Connector

Shorten the triangle base connector. The longer connector occasionally became an obstacle on imperfect launches, and shortening it reduces the chance of the bean bag clipping the frame on the way out.

How we worked.

The team divided ownership across the design cycle naturally and assembly came together cleanly. Reflecting on the project as a whole, there are areas we would change for future builds.

Concept Direction

Colton generated the original ballista concept after research and conversation with the instructor, and set the direction for the team.

Design & Build

Phillip refined the design, drove the dimensioned sketches, the Inkscape file, and produced the actual physical components.

Execution & Assembly

Kwame focused on execution and final assembly, bringing the parts together into a working launch system and managing the test campaign.

What We’d Change

Communication and uneven workload distribution at certain points. Future projects need a clear team lead and a contingency plan, and the experience of working through the shortcomings is what made the project valuable.
